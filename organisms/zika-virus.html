Zika virus

The Zika virus is a Flavivirus from the same family as the Dengue, West Nile, and Yellow fever viruses. It is spread to people through the bite of an infected Aedine mosquito. Zika usually causes a mild fever lasting for several days, and other symptoms include rash, joint pain and conjunctivitis. More worryingly from a public health standpoint is the perceived association of Zika with birth defects (microcephaly) and the progressive neurological disorder Guillain-Barré syndrome.

History & Epidemiology

Zika fever was first documented in the 1950's in the Zika forest of Uganda. Although the virus was thought to be limited to an equatorial belt across Africa and Asia, outbreaks have occurred across the Pacific islands between 2007-2014 and reached South America in 2015. Cases are being reported throughout the Americas from Mexico in the North to Bolivia in the South. A major foci of the current outbreak is Brazil and Colombia where it is estimated that 100,000's of people have been infected. In February 2016 the World Health Organisation (WHO) declared Zika virus to be a global emergency.

Treatment and control

There is currently no vaccine available for Zika virus but for most people the fever will pass within a few days.

Control of mosquito numbers to reduce potential transmission can be addressed on a personal and collective basis. People should reduce the likelihood of being bitten by wearing protective clothing and using repellents (see this CDC factsheet for a good overview). Collectively, effective control of mosquito populations involves aspects of the following: the reduction of potential breeding sites (see this interactive tool from Purdue University), suppression of the mosquito populations via insecticide spraying, sterile insect technique (SIT) or reducing the transmission efficiency of viruses via Wolbachia infection.

Association with Microcephaly and Guillain-Barré syndrome

Zika fever has been associated with a rise in cases of Microcephaly, babies born with smaller heads, and Guillain-Barré syndrome where the body's immune system attacks part of the peripheral nervous system leading in some cases to paralysis. A causal link between the virus and these conditions is not yet proven. Here are links to the relevant WHO factsheets.

The Vectors

The mosquito Aedes aegypti is the principal vector of dengue, chikungunya, yellow fever, and Zika viruses with Ae. albopictus thought to be a minor vector for the same viruses, except for yellow fever were its role has not been proven. More information about these two vectors such as their distribution, life cycle, surveillance and control can be found on the CDC website.

VectorBase contains the following information relating to these species:

Genome

The genomes of Ae. aegypti (Nene et al. 2007) and Ae. albopictus (Chen et al. 2015) are available with our query and analysis tools. The genome assemblies, including the mitochondrial genomes, and predicted gene sets are available from the Downloads section, which also includes annotation in GFF3 format for both species.

NOTE: The Ae. albopictus data will be fully available with the VB-2016-02 release, 23rd February 2016.

Other data types

Further data relating to the species includes:

  • 330,000 single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) calls and 4,000 insertion/deletion (indel) calls for Ae. aegypti (Evans et al. 2015, Faucon et al. 2015, David et al. 2014, Bonizzoni et al. 2013).
  • 24 expression studies for Ae. aegypti from a number of strains including the reference Liverpool (LVP).
  • Insecticide resistance assays and surveillance studies in the population biology section: Ae. aegypti 35 studies and Ae. albopictus 15 studies.
